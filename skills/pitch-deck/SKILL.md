# Pitch Deck

Structure and refine investor pitch decks that tell a compelling story, present the right metrics, and prepare founders for Q&A.

## Slide Structure

A strong pitch deck runs 10-15 slides. The core structure:

1. **Title** -- Company name, one-line description, your name and contact.
2. **Problem** -- What pain exists today? Make it specific and relatable. Use a concrete example or customer quote.
3. **Solution** -- How your product solves the problem. Show the product (screenshot or demo), not just words.
4. **Market** -- TAM, SAM, SOM with bottoms-up justification. Avoid generic "billion-dollar market" claims without substance.
5. **Business Model** -- How you make money. Pricing, revenue streams, unit economics.
6. **Traction** -- MRR, growth rate, customer count, retention, notable logos. This is the most scrutinized slide.
7. **Competition** -- Honest positioning. Use a 2x2 matrix or feature comparison. Show your differentiated advantage.
8. **Go-to-Market** -- How you acquire customers today and how that scales.
9. **Team** -- Founder backgrounds and why this team wins. Highlight relevant domain expertise.
10. **Financials** -- Revenue projections (3 years), burn rate, key assumptions.
11. **Ask** -- How much you are raising, what it funds, and the milestones it enables.

## Storytelling

- Open with the problem, not your company history.
- Use a narrative arc: status quo is broken, here is a better way, here is proof it works, here is where we are going.
- Keep text minimal -- each slide should convey one idea. Use visuals, charts, and data points.
- The deck should stand on its own when forwarded without a presenter.

## Metrics That Matter

Present metrics appropriate to your stage:

- **Pre-seed/Seed** -- Problem validation, early user engagement, waitlist size, pilot results.
- **Series A** -- MRR ($100K+), MoM growth (15%+), retention/NRR, CAC payback.
- **Series B+** -- ARR, path to profitability, NRR > 120%, sales efficiency metrics.

Always show trends (charts over time), not just snapshots.

## Investor Q&A Preparation

Anticipate and prepare concise answers for:

- Why now? What has changed to make this possible or urgent?
- What happens if a big player enters your space?
- What is your biggest risk and how are you mitigating it?
- How did you arrive at your valuation/raise amount?
- What are the key milestones before your next round?
- Why are you the right team to build this?

## Common Mistakes

- Too many slides or too much text per slide.
- Leading with the solution before establishing the problem.
- Showing a TAM slide with no bottoms-up logic.
- Hiding or ignoring weaknesses -- investors will find them.
- Not having a clear ask with a specific use of funds.

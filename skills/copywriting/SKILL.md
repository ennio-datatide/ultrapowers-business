# Copywriting

Write persuasive, clear copy that drives action. Apply proven frameworks to headlines, calls to action, landing pages, and product descriptions.

## The AIDA Framework

Structure copy to move readers through four stages:

1. **Attention** -- Open with a hook that stops the scroll. Use a surprising stat, a bold claim, or a question that speaks to a pain point.
2. **Interest** -- Expand on the problem or opportunity. Show you understand the reader's situation with specific details.
3. **Desire** -- Present the solution and its benefits. Focus on outcomes, not features. Use social proof, testimonials, or data.
4. **Action** -- Close with a clear, single call to action. Tell the reader exactly what to do next.

## Headlines

- Lead with the benefit, not the feature ("Save 4 hours a week" not "Automated scheduling tool").
- Use numbers and specifics ("3 steps to..." outperforms vague promises).
- Keep headlines under 10 words when possible.
- Test headline variants -- small wording changes can shift conversion significantly.

## Calls to Action (CTAs)

- Use action verbs: "Get", "Start", "Download", "Join", "Try".
- Create urgency without being manipulative ("Start your free trial" not "Act now or miss out forever").
- One primary CTA per page or section -- do not split the reader's attention.
- Make the CTA button text describe the outcome ("Get my free report" not "Submit").

## Tone of Voice

- Define tone along four axes: formal vs casual, serious vs playful, respectful vs irreverent, enthusiastic vs matter-of-fact.
- Stay consistent across all touchpoints (website, email, social, support).
- Match the audience's language -- use terms they use, not internal jargon.
- Read copy aloud to check it sounds natural.

## Conversion Copy Principles

- Remove friction: shorter forms, fewer steps, clearer instructions.
- Address objections directly near the CTA (money-back guarantee, no credit card required).
- Use specifics over generalities ("Used by 12,000 teams" not "Trusted by many").
- Write scannable copy: short paragraphs, bullet points, bold key phrases.
- Every sentence should earn its place -- cut anything that does not move the reader forward.

## Common Pitfalls

- Writing about the company instead of the reader -- use "you" more than "we".
- Burying the value proposition below the fold.
- Using passive voice when active voice is more direct.
- Overloading with superlatives ("best", "ultimate", "revolutionary") that erode trust.
